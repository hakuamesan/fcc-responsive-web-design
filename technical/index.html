Sarvangasana

Introduction

In today's life, very few people give proper attention to correct posture. Everyone is used to sitting long hours in front of the computer, either to play games or to study. So what happens to your back ? It becomes stiff. It becomes rigid. There is poor circulation of blood in your body. Your shoulders become hard and stiff like a tree, so much that people might think that you have become a caveman.

How do you solve this problem ? How can you play games for several hours without harming your body ? Once again, as scientists, we turn to our ancient treasures of knowledge to find the answers to our problems. Luckily for us, even though there was no Google 2000+ years ago, even though there was no Internet, the solution to all our problems has been carefully passed on from generation to generation by means of Yoga.

The world of Yoga is as vast as the World of Warcraft and ever bigger, better and more awesome than the levels of Temple Run. It will even crush the Candy Crush! Meaning

The original Sanskrit meaning of these terms are:

Sarva: Every, each Anga: Organs, part of the body Asana: Seat

Some common English names for this asana:

Shoulder stand Inverted stand

This asana is beneficial to each and every part of the body.

Benefits

In this asana, you build up both power and a new structure in your back and you bring relaxation to well-known stress areas like the neck and the lower back. The muscles of your lower back get stronger, the chest and shoulders can move more freely and the back gets straighter. It also stimulates the thyroid gland and is good for blood circulation in the chest, shoulders, upper back and the neck.

This asana stretches the heart muscles by returning more venous blood to the heart, thereby helping people with low blood pressure. This also brings relief to people with varicose veins due to being upside down and hence, easing the flow of blood back to the heart.

In addition, it strengthens the neck muscles and brings a rich supply of blood to the brain. As a preliminary pose to Shirshasana, it accustoms the brain to the increased influx of blood and for the crown to support the weight of the body.

The inverted posture also stimulates the thyroid and parathyroid glands and normalizes their functionsand at the same time tones the legs and abdominal muscles. It also reduces fatigue and alleviates insomnia. It is also considered to be therapeutic for asthma and sinusitis.

Contra-indications

Before we start doing the asana, we need to check who cannot do this asana. If you have a cold or sinusitis, weak spine, neck or back injury, inflammation of the ear, weak eye capillaries, severe near-sightedness, diarrhea, headache, cervical spondylosis or vertigo, please check with your doctor or teacher before doing this asana.

Procedure
  • Lie down on your back on a yoga mat or folded blanket.
  • Check that the head and spine are aligned and that the legs are straight with the feet together.
  • Place the hands beside the body with the palms facing down.
  • Relax the entire body and mind.
  • Contract the abdominal muscles and, with the support of the arms, slowly raise both the legs to the vertical position perpendicular to the body, keeping them straight at 90 degrees.
  • When the legs are vertical, press the arms and hands down on the floor.
  • Slowly and smoothly raise the buttocks and spine off the floor, raising the trunk to a vertical position.
  • As you move your hips upwards, your legs will move back but continue to keep the legs straight and together.
  • Turn the palms of the hands upward, bend the elbows and place the hands behind the ribcage, slightly away from the spine, to support the back. The elbows should be about shoulder width apart.
  • Gently push the chest forward so that it presses firmly against the chin.
  • In the final position, the legs are vertical, together and in a straight line with the trunk. The body is supported by the shoulders, nape of the neck and back of the head. The arms provide stability, the chest rests against the chin and the feet are relaxed.
  • Close the eyes. Relax the whole body in the final pose for as long as is comfortable.
  • To return to the starting position, bring the legs forward until the feet are above and behind the back of the head.
  • Keep the legs straight.
  • Slowly release the position of the hands and place the arms on the floor beside the body with the palms down.
  • Gradually lower each vertebra to the floor, followed by the buttocks, so that the legs resume their initial vertical position.
  • Lower the legs to the floor slowly, keeping the knees straight.
  • The whole movement should combine balance with control so that the body contacts the floor slowly and gently.
  • Relax in shavasana until the respiration and heartbeat return to normal.
  • Practice one of the several counter-pose or follow-up asanas to relax the neck and shoulder muscles.
Variations

If a person is unable to lift the back or lift the legs vertically, he can bend his legs and then use the momentum to push back the legs.

Breathing

Inhale during the starting position. Retain the breath inside while assuming the final pose. Practice slow, deep abdominal breathing in the final pose. Retain the breath inside while lowering the body to the floor.
